Kalman Filter Deep Dive — From Theory to OnStep Commands

Every telescope mount drifts. No motor is perfectly smooth, no gear is perfectly round, no pier is perfectly vertical. The result is that long-exposure images trail, stars elongate, and sub-arcsecond resolution becomes impossible. For a century, astronomers accepted this as a fact of life — buy better gears, spend hours on polar alignment, and hope for the best. HYBRID Telescope Control takes a different approach: predict the drift mathematically, measure the residual error, and correct it in real time. At the center of this system sits the Kalman filter, a 60-year-old algorithm that turns noisy, incomplete measurements into a precise estimate of where your telescope is pointing — and where it will be five seconds from now.

1. The Problem: Why Telescopes Drift

Telescope tracking errors come from everywhere. The worm gear in an equatorial mount introduces periodic error — a sinusoidal wobble that repeats once per worm revolution, typically producing 30 to 120 arcseconds of peak-to-peak deviation. Atmospheric refraction bends starlight, and the amount of bending depends on the star's altitude above the horizon. A star at 30° elevation is refracted 1.7 arcminutes upward; at 10°, the refraction exceeds 5 arcminutes. Temperature changes flex the telescope tube, shifting the focal plane. Wind gusts push the optical tube assembly like a sail. Polar alignment error introduces field rotation that compounds over hours. And the sidereal rate itself — the constant motor speed that tracks the stars — is an open-loop command. It tells the mount where the stars should be, but never checks whether the mount is actually there.

The result is a system with no feedback. The motor runs, the stars drift, and the only way to know if the tracking is working is to take a picture and look at the stars. HYBRID changes this by closing the loop: it plate-solves an image every five seconds, measures the exact pointing error, and sends a correction to the mount before the error can accumulate into visible trailing.

2. What the Kalman Filter Does

The Kalman filter is a recursive estimator. Unlike a batch method that stores all previous measurements and reprocesses them, the Kalman filter keeps only the current state estimate and its uncertainty. Every new measurement updates the estimate, and the old measurements are discarded. This makes it extraordinarily efficient — the computational cost per step is constant, regardless of how long the system has been running.

The position terms capture the current pointing error in arcseconds along the right ascension and declination axes. The velocity terms capture the rate of change — how fast the error is growing or shrinking. Together, these four numbers describe everything the filter needs to know about the current tracking state.

Each correction cycle has two phases. First, the filter predicts: it uses the velocity terms to extrapolate the state forward in time. If the RA error was growing at 0.3 arcseconds per second during the last cycle, the filter predicts it will be 0.3 arcseconds larger in the next cycle. Second, the filter updates: it takes the plate-solve measurement, compares it to the prediction, and blends them together using the Kalman gain. The gain is a weighting factor that determines how much to trust the prediction versus the measurement. If the measurement is noisy — perhaps because thin clouds scattered the starlight — the gain decreases, and the filter leans more on its prediction. If the prediction is wrong — perhaps because a wind gust suddenly pushed the mount — the gain increases, and the filter trusts the measurement more.

This adaptive weighting is what makes the Kalman filter superior to a simple proportional controller. A proportional controller always applies the same correction for the same error. The Kalman filter considers the history of errors, the expected noise, and the system dynamics to produce corrections that are not just reactive but predictive.

The plate-solve step is the only point where the real sky enters the system. Everything else is mathematics. The plate solver identifies stars in a short exposure, matches them against a catalog, and reports how far the telescope is from its intended pointing position. This raw pixel error is then converted to arcseconds using the plate scale — the angular size of each pixel, determined by the telescope's focal length and the camera's pixel size.

The Kalman predict step uses a simple constant-velocity model. Between plate solves, the filter assumes the error will continue to change at the same rate. The predict step also grows the uncertainty covariance P, reflecting the fact that we become less certain about the state as time passes without a measurement. The update step then shrinks that uncertainty by incorporating the new measurement. The Kalman gain K is computed fresh at each step, adapting to the current noise levels and prediction uncertainty.

4. The Correction Math

The filter outputs a four-element state vector in arcseconds. To convert this into a command the mount can execute, HYBRID must translate arcsecond errors into millisecond pulse durations. The ASCOM PulseGuide interface accepts a direction (North, South, East, West) and a duration in milliseconds. The mount firmware interprets these pulses as motor steps at the configured guide rate.

The conversion formula is:

correction_ms = error_arcsec / (15.041 / 3600) / (guide_rate × 0.5)

Here, 15.041 arcseconds per second is the sidereal rate — the speed at which the Earth rotates, and therefore the speed at which a perfectly aligned equatorial mount must track to keep a star centered. The division by 3600 converts arcseconds to degrees, and the guide rate factor (typically 0.5× sidereal) determines how fast the correction pulse moves the mount. The result is the number of milliseconds the motor must run at guide speed to close the measured error.

The velocity terms in the state vector provide an additional advantage: they predict future error. If the RA error is currently 2 arcseconds and growing at 0.4 arcseconds per second, the filter knows that by the time the next plate solve arrives (5 seconds later), the error will be approximately 4 arcseconds. The correction command accounts for this predicted growth, applying a slightly larger pulse than the current error alone would require. This predictive component is what separates Kalman-based tracking from simple proportional-integral-derivative (PID) controllers, which can only react to the current error and must always lag behind a changing signal.

5. Extended Kalman Filter for Nonlinearities

The standard Kalman filter assumes a linear system — the state evolves according to a linear equation, and the measurement is a linear function of the state. For a well-aligned equatorial mount, this assumption holds reasonably well. The tracking error grows approximately linearly over short intervals, and the plate-solve measurement is a direct linear readout of position.

Telescope tracking, however, contains nonlinear terms that the standard filter cannot capture. Atmospheric refraction follows a sec(z) law, where z is the zenith angle. The refraction at 80° altitude is not simply twice the refraction at 60° — the relationship is nonlinear. Alt-azimuth mounts introduce field rotation, where the angle between the camera frame and the sky changes continuously as the mount tracks. Mount geometry errors — flexure, orthogonality, tube sag — produce corrections that depend on the current pointing direction in nonlinear ways.

The Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) handles these nonlinearities by linearizing the system equations at each step using a Jacobian matrix. Instead of using a fixed state transition matrix F, the EKF computes F as the partial derivative of the state transition function with respect to the state, evaluated at the current estimate. This produces a locally linear approximation that captures the first-order nonlinear effects.

HYBRID runs both filters simultaneously. The standard Kalman filter handles equatorial mounts, where the linear assumption is adequate. The EKF handles alt-azimuth mounts, where field rotation and refraction create genuine nonlinear dynamics. The fallback to EKF was added after field testing revealed that the standard filter could not converge on alt-az mounts at low elevation angles, where refraction gradients are steepest.

The Kalman filter doesn't make the mount more precise. It makes the mount aware of its own imperfections — and compensates for them in real time. HYBRID Tracking Philosophy

6. The Matrix Bug That Broke Everything

In the early development of HYBRID's Kalman filter, a single matrix dimension error caused weeks of erratic behavior. The Kalman gain matrix K has dimensions n × m, where n is the number of states (4) and m is the number of measurements (2: RA and Dec). The transpose of K is therefore m × n, or 2 × 4. When the code multiplied the transposed gain by the two-element innovation vector, the result was a two-element vector. But the state update required a four-element vector — one correction for each state component.

The result was an index overflow: the code attempted to write a two-element result into a four-element array, corrupting memory and producing nonsensical state updates. The filter appeared to work when the error was small (because the corruption happened at the end of the array), but diverged wildly when the error was large (because the corrupted values dominated the next prediction step).

The fix was straightforward once identified: the innovation must be computed as a two-element vector (the difference between the two-element measurement and the two-element predicted measurement), then multiplied by the 4×2 gain matrix to produce a four-element state correction. The dimensions must flow consistently: K (4×2) × innovation (2×1) = correction (4×1). Every matrix operation in the filter was audited for dimension consistency, and a validation layer was added that checks dimensions at runtime in debug builds.

7. Real-World Results

The numbers tell the story. Before the Kalman filter, HYBRID's tracking accuracy was limited by the mount's mechanical periodic error: typically 5 to 15 arcseconds RMS, depending on the gear quality and polar alignment. After introducing the Kalman filter, the RMS error dropped to 0.5 to 2 arcseconds — a tenfold improvement that brings long-exposure imaging within reach of modest equipment.

With the machine-learning enhancement that dynamically tunes the filter's noise parameters, sub-arcsecond tracking becomes possible in good conditions. The ML model observes the relationship between predicted and measured errors over time, adjusting the process noise covariance Q and measurement noise covariance R to match the current atmospheric and mechanical conditions. On a steady night with good seeing, the filter converges to extremely tight bounds. On a turbulent night with clouds, it gracefully degrades, leaning more heavily on its predictions and applying smaller, more conservative corrections.

The filter adapts in real time. A wind gust that would have trailed a 30-second exposure is caught within the five-second plate-solve cycle, corrected by a millisecond-scale pulse, and never appears in the final image. A temperature-induced focal shift that would have defocused the stars is predicted by the velocity terms and compensated before it becomes visible. The mount does not become mechanically better. It becomes computationally aware — and that makes all the difference.
